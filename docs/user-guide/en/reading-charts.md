# Reading the Charts

When you open a lot (or a warehouse), FutureKawa shows the **temperature** and
**humidity** measured over time. This page explains how to read those curves.

## The two curves

- The **temperature** curve shows how warm the storage has been, in degrees
  Celsius (°C).
- The **humidity** curve shows how damp the air has been, as a percentage (%).

You can change the **period** shown (for example the last hours, days or weeks) to
zoom in on a recent event or to see a longer trend.

## The ideal band

Each country has an **ideal band** — the range of temperature and humidity in which
green coffee keeps best. The band is drawn directly on the chart so you can see, at
a glance, whether the curve stays inside it.

| Country | Ideal temperature | Ideal humidity | Tolerance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brazil | 29 °C | 55 % | ± 3 °C / ± 2 % |
| Ecuador | 31 °C | 60 % | ± 3 °C / ± 2 % |
| Colombia | 26 °C | 80 % | ± 3 °C / ± 2 % |

The **tolerance** is the acceptable margin around the ideal value. For Brazil, for
example, a temperature between 26 °C and 32 °C is still inside the band.

## Spotting a drift

- **Inside the band** — the curve stays within the shaded ideal range. Conditions
  are good; nothing to do.
- **A single spike** — one brief point outside the band (for example a door left
  open) is usually not a concern on its own.
- **A sustained drift** — the curve leaves the band and **stays out** for a while.
  This is what matters: it means the storage is genuinely too warm, too cold, too
  dry or too damp, and the coffee is at risk. A sustained drift is what triggers an
  **alert**.

When you see a sustained drift, check the [alerts](alerts.md) screen and follow the
expected actions.
